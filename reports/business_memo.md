## 🔴 Bottom Line Up Front

> The 18% sales growth figure presented to the board is misleading. Our analysis shows that the true underlying growth over the same period is closer to **2–3%.** The remaining **~15 percentage points** are explained by seasonal holiday effects — not genuine business expansion.

---

## 1. What We Were Asked to Investigate

The Regional VP presented a figure showing **18% sales growth** over the past two years. The Finance Director raised concerns that this number may not reflect real business performance.

We were asked to investigate whether the growth is real or whether it is being inflated by the natural ups and downs of the retail calendar.

---

## 2. What We Found

We analysed weekly sales data across all **45 Walmart stores** covering **February 2010 to October 2012** — a total of 143 weeks of transaction data.

### 📅 The Seasonal Pattern

The first thing we noticed is that Walmart's sales follow a very predictable pattern every single year:

```
Jan – Oct  →  Sales build steadily throughout the year
November   →  Sharp spike driven by Thanksgiving & Christmas
January    →  Sales drop back as customers recover from holiday spending
```

During holiday weeks, sales run approximately **60% above the normal weekly level.**
After the holidays, sales fall to approximately **20% below the normal level** in January.

This pattern repeated itself in **2010 and 2011 with almost identical intensity.**

---

### ⚠️ The Problem With the 18% Figure

When you compare a **November holiday peak** in one year to a **quieter period** in the previous year, the difference looks enormous — but it has nothing to do with the business growing. It is simply the calendar doing what it always does.

When we strip out these seasonal effects and look only at the true underlying trend, the picture is very different:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                      │
│   Growth claimed by Regional VP   →   ~18%          │
│   True underlying growth found    →   ~2–3%         │
│   Difference (seasonal effect)    →   ~15 pp        │
│                                                      │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

To put this simply — if 18% were real business growth, the **November 2011 holiday spike would be noticeably bigger than the November 2010 spike.** It was not. Both years produced almost exactly the same seasonal uplift, which confirms that the calendar — not business performance — was responsible for the large numbers.

---

## 3. Why This Matters

Growth targets, procurement budgets, and staffing plans built on the 18% figure are based on an inflated baseline. This creates three concrete risks:

| Risk | Consequence |
|---|---|
| 📦 **Inventory orders set too high** | Overstocking, increased carrying costs, product spoilage |
| 🎯 **Revenue targets unrealistic** | Pressure that cannot be met through normal business operations |
| 📊 **Unfair performance evaluations** | Store managers measured against a seasonal illusion, not true growth |

---

## 4. Our Recommendations

### `01` — Reset the Growth Baseline
Future growth should be measured by comparing the **same seasonal periods year over year** — for example, November 2011 against November 2010 — rather than comparing different points in the seasonal cycle. This eliminates the seasonal distortion entirely.

### `02` — Separate Seasonal Performance from Business Performance
Holiday weeks should be tracked and reported **separately** from normal trading weeks. The holiday uplift is real and valuable — but it should be planned for, not mistaken for underlying growth.

### `03` — Revise Next Year's Targets
Any targets built on the 18% figure should be reviewed against the **true trend growth of 2–3%** before they are locked into operational plans.

---

## 5. Closing

> This finding is not a criticism of the Regional VP's intentions. The raw sales numbers do show large increases and it is easy to read them as growth without isolating seasonal effects. The purpose of this memo is to ensure that the business makes its most important planning decisions on **accurate information** rather than a number that looks good on a slide but does not reflect the underlying reality.
